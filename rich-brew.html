Mapping Modern Jewish Cultures

Designing and developing an online companion resource for the 2019 book A Rich Brew: How Cafés Created Modern Jewish Culture

Type Team Project
Role UX Designer & Front-End Developer
Tools Slickplan, Figma, HTML, CSS, Sass, JavaScript, Git
Timespan Currently in progress

Background

As a companion resource to his recently published book A Rich Brew: How Cafés Created Modern Jewish Culture (NYU Press), Professor Shachar Pinsker set out to create a website that would allow people to interact with the book’s information in novel ways. He worked with a team of students to accomplish this. They designed and built a website that possessed interesting features but lacked polish. After it was completed, Prof. Pinsker received feedback from NYU Press pointing out issues with the website’s UX. I was then brought on to the team as a UX Designer to help identify and remedy the website’s shortcomings.

Design

After joining the team, I was brought up to speed with the state of the project. The website was functionally complete; it’s primary features (Cities, People, Time) had been built and its StoryMaps had already been created for the Stories section. However, NYU Press (the book’s publisher) suggested making some improvements to the website’s UX, specifically:


  • Scrolling mechanism - The original website used fullPage.js in order to provide an ‘on-rails’ experience for exploring the content, evocative of the feeling of reading a book. However, this also produced a choppy scrolling effect that made exploration slow and difficult. We decided that the new website needed to have a smoother scrolling effect so that users could explore more freely.
  • Stories page - The aforementioned full-page scrolling effect made browsing the various StoryMaps available on the Stories page difficult. Because each StoryMap thumbnail occupied the entire viewport, users could only view one at a time. Therefore, if a user wanted to access the 10th StoryMap on the page, they had to scroll down 9 times. This required significant effort from users to browse through all of the StoryMaps available.
  • Home page - The existing home page contained a nice block of introductory text that effectively conveyed the purpose of the website (section 1).

However, the remaining sections of the home page didn’t provide effective forward paths for users to begin exploring the website’s tools and content. For example, the next section (section 2) used the entire viewport to provide instructions for using the website.

It wasn’t until the third section of the home page (requiring users to scroll down twice) that users were finally presented with forward paths to the website’s primary features. Additionally, the links in this section didn’t directly correspond to the labels in the navigation bar (e.g. ‘guided story’ vs. ‘Stories’).


  • Navigation bar- The existing navigation bar was functional but lacked clear organization and recognizable labels (e.g. ‘Start here’ linked to the home page). While Cities, People, and Time all function as complementary tools, utilizing the same database, Stories is a markedly different feature, containing StoryMaps content that isn’t shared by any of the website’s other features.

    Navigation bar



As the team’s new UX Designer, it was my job to resolve these issues. In order to gain a better understanding of the design decisions that resulted in the current website, I reached out to the website’s original designer. The original website was designed and built entirely by one team member who’s experience lied primarily in software development. They were forthcoming about what they believed to be the design’s shortcomings and provided valuable insight into the team’s process for creating the website. I continued to communicate with them throughout the redesign process.

By synthesizing information about the issues that we identified, I formed two conclusions:

  • There was an opportunity to improve the website’s structure so that it better reflects the relation between the various sections.
  • As discussed earlier, the full-page scrolling mechanism negatively impacted the website’s overall UX; however, it also contributed to the specific issues that were identified on the Stories and Home pages.

These conclusions helped clarify the problems that our team was attempting to solve and enabled us to begin acting on them.

In order to improve the website’s structure, I created a sitemap that included a clear distinction between the Cities, People, and Time pages and the Stories page. This was accomplished by introducing an Explore landing page that grouped the Cities, People, and Time pages together. My intention was to simplify the website’s navigation while introducing a structure that better reflects the relation between its different sections.

Wireframes
After creating a new sitemap, I proceeded to produce a set of low-fidelity wireframes for both the new and existing pages of the website. Due to its importance, I began with the home page.

Home page
The home page needed to concisely convey the purpose and context of the website while providing clear forward paths to its primary features.





For the home page’s wireframe, I attempted to do the following:

  • Simplify the navigation bar by consolidating the Cities, People, and Time navigation links into a single dropdown menu. I also changed Start here to Home since the latter is more familiar to users.
  • Place the primary features' forward paths in their own distinct sections and provide context regarding their respective purposes. This would allow users to distinguish between the various features and better understand what they each offer.
  • Communicate how to use the website through the home page’s design rather than explicit text instructions. This would also allow us to remove the original full-page set of instructions featured on the page.


Footer
I worked with Prof. Pinsker to determine what information the footer needed to display. He decided that it should include acknowledgements for the project’s funding sources, and I suggested that we include contact information as well as a sitemap.






Explore
The Explore page needed to act as a simple landing page that would lead users to the Cities, People, and Time features. Because this was a new page, there wasn’t any existing content that needed to be included.





In designing the Explore page, I tried to emphasize simplicity. It only needed to provide forward paths and additional context for the Cities, People, and Time features. Because all three features are complementary and equally important, none of them are given prominence over the others.

Stories
The new Stories page needed to facilitate users browsing through the variety of StoryMaps available. Since the original website’s use of fullPage.js made this difficult, my first step in redesigning the page was to reduce the size of the StoryMaps’s thumbnails considerably so that several of them could fit within the viewport.





With the new design, StoryMap titles are displayed in a semi-transparent black bar at the bottom of the thumbnails. When users move their cursor over a thumbnail, its black bar extends over the remainder of the thumbnail and displays a brief description of the StoryMap. In designing this effect, my intention was to utilize progressive disclosure to provide users with just enough information to decide which StoryMap they would like to explore.


Prototyping
After creating the low-fidelity wireframes, I created a HTML/CSS prototype that reflected the changes I was proposing. Over the course of a week, I quickly created a semi-responsive HTML/CSS prototype using the static site generator Jekyll. I hosted the website on GitHub Pages and shared it with the rest of the team, asking for feedback. As a team, we reviewed the prototype and identified possible opportunities for improvement. After our review, we decided that we were ready to move forward and begin fleshing out the wireframes.


High-Fidelity Mockups
Around this time, we brought on another team member with a background in graphic design. Due to my lack of experience with color, typography, and branding, I felt much more comfortable having a visual designer assist us in bringing the low-fidelity wireframes to high-fidelity mockups. I worked with them to tweak some aspects of the UI while they worked with Prof. Pinsker to determine how the website’s aesthetic should reflect that of the book.

The visual designer and I were able to collaborate on creating the high-fidelity mockups through Figma’s real-time collaboration features. While they handled decisions regarding visual details, I provided guidance on how the website should function and feel. Once we produced something that we both liked, we presented the results to the rest of the team.

Home page



- Header & Navigation
The visual designer I worked with had the idea of using one large image for the page’s background and only having it appear in the page’s header and footer. The effect this created was visually appealing and communicated to users that they reached the bottom (or top) of the page. The visual designer suggested centering the website title and navigation to keep a more familiar, conventional look.

- About
By reflecting on the original set of wireframes I created, I decided that we could combine the About section’s text and the introductory block of text. Since the two sections served a similar purpose (to inform the user about the project and provide a forward path to the About page), combining these sections eliminated redundant content and simplified the flow of the home page.

- Explore
The visual designer on our team was able to enhance the presentation of the Explore section by adjusting the layout and including large, engaging imagery to represent the different Explore features.

- Stories
During our review of the prototype, our team decided that the featured stories displayed in the Stories section would ultimately work better in a 1x4 row than a 2x2 square. We felt that this would better communicate to users that there are more stories available, since the row could conceivably extend indefinitely (as the screen width increases) whereas the square is self-contained.




Explore
The Explore page mockup that we presented to the team was nearly an exact reflection of the page’s original wireframe. Although the visual designer and I felt that this was an effective design for the page, some other members of the team felt that the page should place a greater emphasis on the images. The visual designer and I reflected on this feedback and modified the page’s design so that it better emphasized the images.

Stories
The high-fidelity mockup of the Stories page remained largely the same as the low-fidelity prototype. However, one key change is the removal of the black, hover-activated summary windows for each of the stories. During our review of the prototype, Prof. Pinsker said that he liked the idea but did not have the time required to create summaries for each of the stories.
