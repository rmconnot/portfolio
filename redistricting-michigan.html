Empowering Citizens to Redistrict Michigan

Researching and designing a tool that enables Michigan citizens to guide the state's redistricting process.

Type Team Project
Role Product Designer and User Researcher
Tools Balsamiq, Figma, Google Maps
Timespan Fall 2019 | 14 weeks

Background

In Fall 2019 I participated in the University of Michigan Design Clinic program. Design Clinic pairs groups of students with local clients who can benefit from the program’s focus on problem solving through design thinking.


My group was paired with Voters Not Politicians (VNP), a pro-democracy organization that was instrumental in passing Proposal 2 (2018). The proposal effectively ended gerrymandering in Michigan by establishing an Independent Redistricting Commission. Importantly, one of its key components is the requirement for the commission to consider citizens’ input throughout the redistricting process. The first Independent Redistricting Commission will be formed in Summer 2020.


The Problem

In other states that have passed similar legislation (California and Arizona), the redistricting commissions did not have formal processes in place for citizens to provide input. This led to the commissions receiving input in a variety of formats (emails, physical mail, speeches at public hearings). Because the input varied so greatly in format, it also varied in usefulness to the commmissions' redistricting efforts.


VNP is seeking to preemptively provide a more effective means for the commission to collect citizens’ input, so that Michiganders could ultimately have a greater, more meaningful impact on the state’s redistricting process.


VNP approached Design Clinic to help them explore different ways in which citizens could share their input, and ultimately recommend a system to them that would facilitate this sharing.

Scoping

Our team quickly scheduled an introductory meeting with our primary stakeholder, a senior member of VNP. During our first meeting we began scoping the project, a process that would continue over the following weeks. During this process, our team was attempting to accomplish several things:


  • Set achievable expectations for the project
  • Determine what the actual problem we needed to solve was
  • Ensure that our final recommendations (whatever they may be) would be actionable by the client

Due to the sheer magnitude of the problem we were trying to solve, the scoping phase lasted nearly a month and continued to be re-addressed throughout the course of our time working on the project. Ultimately, we were able to produce a concise problem statement that allowed us to begin solving the problem:


"Michigan’s Independent Redistricting Commission needs an effective system for collecting citizens’ input about their communities of interest in order to draw district lines that preserve the integrity of these communities."


After settling on an actionable problem statement, we worked with our client to determine the course of the project as well as what deliverables our would ultimately provide. This process involved evaluating how effective various forms of deliverables would be in helping us address our problem statement.

Research

The next phase in the course of our project was research. Because of the considerable domain knowledge this project required, we knew that user research alone would not be sufficient. Therefore we divided the research duties into the following categories:


  • Background research regarding the redistricting processes of states who have passed similar legislation (California and Arizona)... essentially ”what worked and what didn’t?”
  • Product research evaluating the variety of digital redistricting tools currently available
  • User research in order to learn more about who the system’s potential users are and how they might be affected by its introduction
  • Usability research that helped us understand what users’ optimal method of interacting with a redistricting system might look like. This research also illuminated a myriad of issues with existing solutions as well as our early design prototypes.

I chose to take on responsibilities for the product research and usability research while the other members of my team assumed responsibility for the other research duties.


Product Research

My product research involved first identifying which tools are currently available that would suit VNP’s purposes. I reached out to several redistricting experts to learn which tools they recommended using. I also searched for tools on my own through simple Google searches and reading relevant articles.


After identifying a number of tools, I created a set of criteria I could use in order to evaluate their suitability for VNP. The three main criteria were:


  • Cost - our primary stakeholder stressed this as a major concern from our first meeting
  • Degree of customization required - an out-of-the-box solution would be ideal; unfortunately, it doesn’t yet exist
  • Method of map-drawing - some tools allow users to draw free-form polygons while others only allow users to highlight pre-drawn precincts

Based on these criteria, I suggested that VNP consider using the APIs for either Districtr, Google Maps, or ArcGIS. (During our final meeting with our client, they confirmed that they will be using Google Maps.)


Usability Research

Once I had settled on three different applications which we would be recommending to VNP, I collaborated with another team member to conduct guerilla usability testing. Because Districtr offered a fundamentally different method of indicating location (highlighting precincts) than Google Maps or ArcGIS (drawing free-form polygons), I wanted to better understand how users interact with each of these methods. Therefore, I resolved to use Districtr and the Google Maps (specifically the My Maps feature) for the usability tests, due to their contrasting methods of location indication.


Our usability tests consisted of approaching strangers in public areas such as libraries, campus buildings, and coffee shops and asking if they would be willing to spend ~10 minutes using two mapping applications If they agreed, then we briefly introduced them to the first application on a laptop and simply asked them to “please indicate the location of your neighborhood, using the tools available to you.” We provided minimal guidance and made note of issues users encountered. After they had succeeded or given up, we introduced them to the second application and asked them to do the same thing. After the user was finished with the second application, we debriefed them, asking open ended questions about their experience with both applications (what they liked, what they didn’t like, which tool they preferred, why) and thanked them for their time.



These usability tests provided us with a number of key insights:


  • Users prefer to enter a location into a search bar instead of manually zooming in to find it. However, many users did not use the search bars provided by the two applications we tested. Initially, we were confused why this was occurring; however, we eventually discovered that many of our users simply didn’t notice the search bars when they began using the applications. We decided that our team’s design needed to feature a prominent search bar that users could immediately identify.
  • Users struggled to locate each application’s respective tools for actually “marking” or indicating a geographic area. These tools tended to be given little prominence in the applications’ UIs, often being placed in a small toolbar near the corners of the window. Again, we decided that our team’s design needed to lend greater prominence to this UI element.
  • Most users felt that the precincts laid out in Districtr mapped onto their actual neighborhoods with fairly high accuracy. When asked about their precinct’s accuracy, several users said, “it’s pretty close to how I would have drawn it”.

With the product and usability research complete, I documented our findings in a preliminary report and shared them with the team during our weekly team meeting. As other team members completed their research duties, they shared their findings as well. As a team, we prepared to move on to the design phase of the project.


One key decision that was made by our group during this period was that the system we were designing would take the form of a website, or online portal, that would enable users to “draw” and submit a map indicating the locations of their communities of interest as well as descriptions of the respective communities. In making this decision, we were attempting to balance ease-of-use for citizens with the usefulness of the information to the Independent Redistricting Commission. In other words, the system that we were designing needed to be simple and easy for citizens to use, but it also needed to provide valuable information for the commission for their redistricting purposes. We felt that an online portal struck a good balance between these two interests.

Design

Earlier, while determining the course of the project, our team agreed to provide VNP with the following deliverables:


  • Task flow
  • Low-fidelity wireframes
  • Medium-fidelity prototype

Around this time, we decided that we could collaborate on these design deliverables as a team. Since everyone worked on different aspects of the research, we wanted to ensure that nobody’s perspective was left out from the actual design.


Task flow

With the task flow, we tried to make it as simple as possible, only requiring the steps that were truly necessary. The logic behind several of the steps will become clear through the design.





Low-Fidelity Wireframes


We began the process of creating our wireframes with a group whiteboarding session. During this session, we listed the elements that our design needed to include and began sketching rough wireframes that communicated how we intended for citizens to use the website.


Next, one of our group members began translating these sketches into a Balsamiq design file. I collaborated with her to flesh out the wireframes and make adjustments.


During this time, I proposed making the map “drawing” step optional for users who would rather not submit a map. This suggestion was informed by both the usability research and user research that our team had conducted, both of which indicated that many users are not fully comfortable or willing to use a mapping application in order to provide input about their communities. With this proposed change, users would only be required to submit a written description of their communities; the map would be optional.


The team agreed with this proposal and we created an alternate version of the original wireframe. We then presented both versions to the client in order to receive feedback regarding the usefulness of a map-less submission to the Independent Redistricting Commission. Our client agreed that the map-drawing step could and probably should be optional, so we pursued the alternate version.



Home page

Through our research about Michigan, we learned two things:

  • Michigan is a relatively diverse state, with a sizeable populations of those for whom Spanish and Arabic are their primary languages.
  • Most Michigan citizens are not familiar with Proposal 2, the redistricting process, or even gerrymandering in general.

In our design of the homepage, we addressed these realities by including relevant language options as well as introductory text about the redistricting process and why the user’s input is important.

Communities of Interest page
Description page

Unsuprisingly, our user research found that because of the ambiguity of the term “community of interest”, many people struggle to understand what it is that we’re asking for. Therefore, we included an introductory section that defines what is meant by “community of interest”.

Tutorial page
Mapping page

Our usability research found that many users encountered difficulty when trying to “draw” their community on a map. Therefore, we included an instructional page that demonstrates how to use the mapping tools provided. This also allows users to get a taste of the mapping process and decide if it’s a step they would rather skip.

Contact Info page
Success page

Through our user research, we found that many people, especially those in historically marginalized communities, were hesitant to provide personal information in a political context. Therefore, we tried to make sure that we only required them to provide the minimum amount of personal information necessary for the commission to verify that the submission came from an actual Michigan resident.


After we finished creating our low-fidelity wireframes, we presented them to our client and shared them with the mentor that had been assigned to our team at the beginning of the semester. We received a lot of great feedback and proceeded to create the medium-fidelity prototype.


Medium-Fidelity Prototype

With our team’s medium-fidelity prototype, we set out to simplify the submission process even further while refining the UI elements we had introduced in the low-fidelity wireframes.



Home page

Through further usability testing with our low-fidelity wireframes, we found that the submission process took slightly more than ten minutes to complete, on average; we reflected in the new homepage. One piece of important feedback that we received from our team’s mentor was that “there are really only 3 steps involved, some of the steps just have an extra page”. This led us to present the submission process as a ‘3-step process’ that users could more easily understand. We were also provided with some copy that we could use to provide context.




Community of Interest page

The purpose of the community of interest page is to help users understand what constitutes a Community of Interest. Through our user testing, we found that people struggled to understand the term ‘Communities of Interest’. Therefore, we decided to include a page where users can get a better understanding of the term.




Description page

We decided to keep the description page as simple as possible. We based the four questions we asked on an explanatory handout developed by UC Berkeley’s Law Redistricting Group for California’s Redistricting Commission.




Description page

In order to further simplify the submission process, we removed the tutorial page from the list of steps in the low-fidelity wireframes. Instead, we provide a link to an external webpage that presents the video tutorial in case users need help understanding how to draw a map.




Submission page

One of the public hearings that we attended had a member of California’s redistricting commission present to discuss their process. One important thing she mentioned was that after a citizen submitted their input, the commission would often contact citizens to clarify certain aspects of their submission. Therefore, we found it important to require contact information from users so that the Commission can contact them with any questions. For now, we used physical address and email address to authenticate that the person submitting the form is a real human. In the design we placed a question mark next to physical address, so that VNP can include information as to why a physical address was being asked in the first place. Users may want to know the exact purpose of providing this information.


Underneath the contact information page we give users the opportunity to review their submission before pressing “Submit”. We believed that allowing users to receive a copy of their submission via email would be beneficial.



Success page

Conclusion

We presented our final design along with our findings and other deliverables to our client in the format of a 43-page report as well as a formal in-person presentation. They were pleased with the results and thanked us for our work on the project. Shortly after our presentation, they informed us that they have found a software engineering firm to continue developing the website. VNP shared our final report with the firm and they have reached out to several members of our group with questions about the project. Looking forward, the firm will continue developing the website so that it is ready for the Independent Redistricting Commission in Summer 2020, when the commission will be formed. I plan to provide input through the website when it’s ready!


Looking back, there were several areas of the project that I wish we could have done differently. Most importantly, given the time constraints of the project (1 semester), I wish we could have spent less time scoping the project and more time conducting user research and usability testing. Our team’s relative inexperience with client work and the inherent complexity of the project led to the scoping phase lasting slightly longer than was absolutely necessary. If we had more time, we could have conducted more usability tests and user interviews.


I was very fortunate to have had the opportunity to work on such an important and impactful project. Additionally, I couldn’t have asked for a better group to work with. I was lucky to have several talented and hardworking team members that made the project a valuable learning experience.
